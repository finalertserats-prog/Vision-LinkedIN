Here's a question I can't put down: should a hospital tell you about a mistake you'd never have noticed?

My gut said yes, obviously. Always. The moment an institution decides which of its errors you're "entitled" to know, it has made itself the judge of your truth — and that's exactly the power you shouldn't trust anyone to hold in secret. The harmless slip is the real test. Telling you about the mistake that hurt you is damage control. Telling you about the one you'd never catch is the only proof honesty is a standing commitment and not a liability calculation.

But watching the argument unfold, I noticed something. Even the fiercest "tell everything" voices flinched at one line — the near-miss. The dose caught before it reached you. The wrong chart spotted and fixed in seconds. Dump every internal hiccup on a patient and you haven't given them honesty; you've given them anxiety, and handed the hospital a clever disguise for self-absolution: "look how transparent we are."

So the honest rule isn't "tell everything" or "use your judgment." It's narrower and harder: if it touched your body, your record, your risk, your options, your cost — you're told, full stop, no matter how small. If it never reached you, that's the system debugging itself, not a secret kept from you.

The line isn't drawn at how big the mistake was. It's drawn at whether it ever became yours.
