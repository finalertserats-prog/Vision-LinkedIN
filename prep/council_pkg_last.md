TOPIC: Is nostalgia a comforting lie we tell ourselves, or a genuine survival tool?

Here's the contrarian read: the thing people mock as sentimental weakness might be one of the most functional tricks the mind ever evolved.

The council split hard on nostalgia this week.

One voice made the prosecution's case, and made it well: nostalgia is a comforting lie. It quietly deletes the boredom, the dread, the actual struggle — and hands you a fictional golden age. Live there long enough and you start resenting the present for the crime of being difficult. A sedative that numbs the very instincts you need to adapt.

The rebuttal was sharper than I expected. Yes, nostalgia edits the past — but ALL memory edits. The question isn't accuracy, it's function. The brain isn't running a documentary; it's building a usable model: "life can be good, I have belonged, I can belong again." It hands you that model precisely when you're lonely, aging, or afraid. Not escapism — psychological fuel. And the research points the same way: nostalgia raises approach motivation. It mobilizes; it doesn't sedate.

What stayed with me is where the fight actually lands. Nostalgia doesn't delete the pain — it reframes it as survived. "I came through that" is exactly the signal that lets you face the next hard thing.

A lie is told to deceive. This one is told to endure.

The distortion isn't the bug. It might be the whole point.
