The comfort of being slightly misunderstood may be more sustainable than the exhaustion of being fully known.
7 Jul 2026
STATUS
1,430c
Proposed post
I run a hospital. Try being "slightly misunderstood" when a family is deciding whether to trust you with someone they love.

There's a comfortable idea going around: that it's easier to be a little misread than fully known. Keep a margin of mystery, protect your interior, don't exhaust yourself explaining. I get the appeal. Being fully known by the wrong people, or too many at once, is its own kind of tax.

But I watched three sharp arguments circle this and quietly swap one word for another. They defended *privacy* — the parts of yourself you simply haven't shown yet — and called it *being misunderstood*. Those aren't the same thing. One is a door you haven't opened. The other is a wrong portrait of you hanging in someone else's head, that you now spend energy maintaining.

Here's what none of them said out loud: in most real work, you don't get to choose. A patient's family, a staff member on their worst day, a regulator — they will read you fast and act on it. "Slightly misunderstood" isn't a cozy sanctuary there. It's a chart with the wrong allergy on it.

Mystery is a luxury of low stakes. The higher the stakes, the more being accurately read — as far as you've gone — beats being comfortably misread. Unfinished is fine. Wrong is expensive.

Maybe comfort isn't the goal. Maybe it's being legible where it counts, and unbothered everywhere else.
